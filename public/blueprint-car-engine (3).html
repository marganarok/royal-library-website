Evolution of Engine Technology

1769
James Watt patents the steam engine, laying the foundation for mechanical power. The first steam-powered road vehicles appear, though impractical for widespread use.
1859
Jean Joseph Etienne Lenoir develops the first practical internal combustion engine using coal gas. This marks the beginning of the petroleum engine era.
1876
Nikolaus Otto invents the four-stroke cycle engine, revolutionizing internal combustion technology and making automotive transportation feasible.
1885
Karl Benz patents the first automobile powered by an internal combustion engine. The Benz Patent-Motorwagen features a single-cylinder engine producing 0.75 hp.
1908
Henry Ford introduces the Model T with a simple, reliable four-cylinder engine. Mass production techniques make automobiles accessible to the general public.
1954
Felix Wankel patents the rotary engine, offering high power density and smooth operation. Mazda later adopts this technology for their sports cars.
1962
The first turbocharged production car engine appears. Turbochargers become essential for high-performance and fuel-efficient engines.
1980s
Fuel injection systems replace carburetors, providing better fuel efficiency and performance. Computer-controlled engine management becomes standard.
1990s
Variable valve timing and cylinder deactivation technologies emerge, optimizing engine efficiency across different operating conditions.
2010s
Hybrid powertrains and electrification begin to challenge traditional internal combustion engines. Turbocharged downsized engines become prevalent.
